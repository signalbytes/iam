Smart Home

The $30 Device That Can Change Your Mornings

Everyday Tech6 min readApril 2025

Key Takeaways

  • A smart plug costs around £25–£35 and needs no technical knowledge to set up
  • You can put lamps, coffee makers, heaters, or fans on automatic schedules
  • Most smart plugs work with Alexa, Google Home, and Apple HomeKit out of the box
  • You don't need a full smart home system — one plug is a complete, standalone solution
  • Automation only takes five to ten minutes to configure through a smartphone app
  • Smart plugs won't work safely with high-draw appliances like ovens or washing machines

Most people hear "smart home" and picture a house wired top to bottom with sensors, hubs, and monthly subscriptions. That's one version. There's a quieter version that costs less than a restaurant dinner and takes fifteen minutes to set up.

What a Smart Plug Actually Is

A smart plug is a small adapter that goes between your wall socket and whatever you've plugged into it. It looks like a standard travel adaptor. The difference is that it connects to your Wi-Fi and gives you remote control over the power — through your phone, a schedule, or a voice command.

That's the whole thing. No installation, no wiring, no complicated setup. You plug it in, download the companion app (usually a few megabytes), connect it to your home Wi-Fi, and you're done. Most people are up and running in under ten minutes.

What You Can Actually Do With It

The most common use is the morning lamp. Instead of waking up to darkness and fumbling for a switch, your bedside or living room light can turn on automatically — say, fifteen minutes before your alarm. By the time you're awake, the room is already lit. It sounds small. It doesn't feel small.

The same logic applies to a coffee maker with a manual switch. Plug it into a smart plug, set a schedule for 7:10 AM, and your coffee is ready when you walk into the kitchen. No app interaction needed once it's configured.

Other Things Worth Automating

Beyond mornings, smart plugs are genuinely useful for a few other situations:

  • A heater or fan that runs for an hour before you get home from work
  • A phone charger that cuts off at midnight so you're not overcharging overnight
  • A desk lamp that turns off at 10 PM as a gentle reminder to wind down
  • Holiday lighting that you can turn on and off from abroad

None of these require any technical knowledge. You're essentially just setting a timer — the same kind of timer that exists on an oven, except you can change it from your phone.

Do Voice Assistants Make This Better?

They do, but they're optional. If you already have an Alexa device, a Google Nest speaker, or use Apple's Siri, most smart plugs connect to them automatically during setup. After that, you can say "turn off the lamp" from another room instead of reaching for your phone.

It's a convenience layer, not a requirement. The scheduled automations work fine without any voice assistant at all.

"A smart plug isn't about technology for its own sake. It's about removing the smallest, most forgettable tasks from your morning — so your attention goes somewhere better."

What Smart Plugs Won't Do

Worth being direct about the limits. Smart plugs are rated for a maximum wattage — usually around 2,400W in the UK and Europe. That covers most lamps, coffee makers, phone chargers, fans, and small heaters.

They are not suitable for high-draw appliances: ovens, washing machines, dishwashers, or tumble dryers. Using a smart plug with these is a safety risk. The packaging will tell you the maximum load — check it before plugging anything in.

Smart plugs also need a working Wi-Fi connection. If your router goes down, the schedule still works (it's stored on the device), but remote control through the app won't. Not a problem for most people; worth knowing if your broadband is unreliable.

Which One to Buy

The market is crowded. For most people in the UK, the TP-Link Tapo range (around £12–£20 per plug) and the Amazon Smart Plug (around £25) are reliable starting points. Both have well-rated apps, good reliability records, and work with all three major voice assistants.

If you have an iPhone and use Apple devices, the Eve Energy plug (around £35) works with Apple Home natively, without needing a separate hub or cloud account.

There's no need to buy a multipack to start. One plug, one device, one small change. See whether it makes your mornings any easier before committing to more.

Getting comfortable with any new technology tends to start with a single, low-stakes experiment. A smart plug is about as low-stakes as it gets — but the habit it builds, of letting small automations handle small tasks, can quietly make a real difference over time.

How to Write a Difficult Email in Half the Time

Everyday Tech5 min readApril 2025

Key Takeaways

  • AI writing tools can help you get past the blank page — but you'll still need to edit the output
  • They work best for emails that are awkward, formal, or emotionally difficult to start
  • You stay in control: the AI drafts, you decide what gets sent
  • Free tools like Claude.ai or ChatGPT require no subscription to get started
  • AI writing assistants cannot read the relationship context behind an email — that judgement remains yours

There's a specific kind of email that everyone puts off. The complaint to a supplier. The apology to a colleague. The request to a manager that you've rewritten four times and still aren't satisfied with. AI writing tools exist precisely for moments like these.

Why People Find Certain Emails Hard

The difficulty usually isn't the content — you know what you want to say. It's the framing. How direct is too direct? Is this too formal, or not formal enough? Will this come across as passive-aggressive if I phrase it that way?

These questions create a kind of paralysis. The email sits in your drafts folder for two days while you think about it. An AI assistant doesn't remove the judgment — that's still yours — but it removes the paralysis by giving you something to react to rather than something to create from nothing.

How the Process Actually Works

The practical workflow is straightforward. You open a free AI chat tool — Claude, ChatGPT, and Gemini all work for this — and you describe the email you need to write. The more specific you are, the more useful the output.

A vague prompt like "write a professional email" produces a generic email. A specific prompt produces something genuinely usable. Here's the difference in practice:

A Vague Prompt

"Write a professional email to my manager about working from home."

This will get you something technically correct but tonally bland — the kind of email that reads like it was written by a corporate policy document.

A Useful Prompt

"I need to ask my manager if I can work from home two days a week. The context is that we haven't spoken about flexible working before, and I want to make the request without sounding demanding. I work in marketing. Please write a draft that's polite and suggests a trial period."

That second prompt produces a draft you can actually use as a starting point. You'll still edit it — adjust the tone, add a specific detail, change a phrase that doesn't sound like you — but you've cut the hard part: the blank page.

"The AI is doing the structural work. The sentence that starts the conversation, the logical order of points, the closing line. You're doing the human work — deciding whether it's right."

When This Approach Works Best

The genuinely useful cases are the ones that cost you the most time and mental energy:

  • Complaint emails to companies where you want to be firm without being rude
  • Follow-ups to people who haven't responded and you don't want to seem pushy
  • Formal requests to people with more authority than you
  • Difficult personal messages where you need help organising your thoughts
  • Emails in a language that isn't your first

For routine emails — a quick confirmation, a meeting time, a thank you note — AI assistance adds more friction than it removes. These are faster to write directly.

What These Tools Don't Know

AI writing tools have no knowledge of the relationship behind your email. They don't know that your manager appreciates bluntness, or that this client is sensitive about deadlines, or that the tone you use with your landlord needs to be very precise given a difficult history.

This is why every AI-generated draft needs human review. The tool produces a draft; you decide whether it's appropriate. Think of it the way you might think of a helpful colleague who doesn't know all the context — you'd ask them for a draft, then check it carefully before sending.

Also worth noting: AI drafts can sound slightly formal or slightly generic. They often need a sentence or two adjusted to sound like an actual person. That editing step is brief, and it's important.

Where to Start

Claude.ai, ChatGPT, and Google's Gemini are all free to use at a basic level. No account is needed for initial access on some platforms. Open any of them, describe your email situation honestly and specifically, and ask for a draft. Read it. Edit it. Send what feels right.

If you find the first draft isn't quite right, you can reply to the AI with feedback — "make it less formal" or "remove the paragraph about the deadline" — and it will revise. This back-and-forth usually takes two or three exchanges at most before you have something usable.

Writing assistance has existed for a long time — style guides, grammar checkers, dictation software. AI email drafting is the latest version of a very old idea: getting help with the words so you can focus on what you actually mean to say.
Digital Wellbeing

Your Phone Already Has a Stress‑Relief Button

Everyday Tech5 min readApril 2025

Key Takeaways

  • Screen Time (iPhone) and Digital Wellbeing (Android) are built into every modern smartphone and free to use
  • Focus Mode lets you silence specific apps without switching your phone off completely
  • App limits don't delete apps — they just add a pause before you can keep scrolling
  • You can schedule downtime periods to automatically reduce interruptions in the evenings
  • These tools work best when set up with realistic limits, not overly strict ones

Somewhere in your phone's settings, there's a set of tools designed specifically to help you use your phone less. They've been there for years. Most people have never opened them.

What These Tools Are

Apple calls it Screen Time. Android calls it Digital Wellbeing. Both are built-in features — no download required — that track how long you spend on your phone, show you which apps are consuming the most of your attention, and let you set limits or scheduled quiet periods.

They exist because the companies that make smartphones have heard, repeatedly, that people feel like their phones own them rather than the other way around. These features are an acknowledgment of that problem.

The Three Most Useful Features

1. App Limits

You can set a daily time limit for specific apps — say, 45 minutes on Instagram or one hour across all news apps. When you reach the limit, the app icon goes grey and a message appears asking whether you want to ignore the limit or stop for the day.

It's not a hard block. You can always choose to ignore it. But that moment of friction — the pause, the choice — is often enough to make you put the phone down. The limit isn't there to punish you. It's there to interrupt the habit loop before you've scrolled for twenty minutes without noticing.

2. Focus Mode

Focus Mode (iPhone) and Focus Mode in Digital Wellbeing (Android) lets you create custom profiles for different parts of your day. A "Work" focus might silence all notifications except calls and your calendar. A "Wind Down" focus might silence everything except messages from specific people.

You can activate these manually, on a schedule, or — on iPhone — triggered automatically when you arrive at a location. Many people find a simple evening focus mode (7 PM to 8 AM) genuinely changes how they feel at the end of the day. Fewer interruptions. Less compulsive checking.

3. Downtime

Downtime lets you schedule a period each day — typically evenings and early mornings — when most apps become inaccessible. Only phone calls and the apps you specifically whitelist (clock, maps, camera) remain available.

This is the most assertive of the three features. It's worth using only if you've found that lighter approaches — app limits, focus mode — haven't changed your habits much.

"These tools don't reduce your phone to a dumb device. They add a layer of intention — a small pause between impulse and action."

Setting This Up on iPhone

Go to Settings → Screen Time. If you've never used it, tap "Turn On Screen Time" and follow the brief setup. From there: App Limits lets you set per-app daily limits. Downtime lets you schedule quiet periods. Focus is accessible from Settings → Focus, where you can edit the default profiles (Do Not Disturb, Work, Personal) or create your own.

Setting This Up on Android

Go to Settings → Digital Wellbeing & Parental Controls. The Dashboard shows your daily app usage. Tap any app to set a timer. Bedtime Mode and Focus Mode are both listed in this section. The exact location varies slightly by manufacturer — Samsung devices list it under Settings → Wellbeing, for example.

What Actually Works

The honest answer: starting with one small change is more effective than overhauling everything at once. A single 30-minute limit on your most-used app, combined with a Do Not Disturb schedule after 9 PM, tends to be a more sustainable starting point than locking down your entire phone.

The goal isn't to use your phone less as a moral victory. It's to use it more deliberately — so the time you do spend on it is time you've chosen, not time that slipped away.

One realistic expectation: these limits won't work if you routinely override them within seconds. That's a habit change, which is a separate and slower process. The technology gives you the structure. The rest is up to you.

The most effective technology tools are often the ones that get out of your way. The irony of digital wellbeing features is that they require a few minutes of deliberate setup — but then they quietly do their job in the background, every day, without you having to think about them again.

Seven Dinners, Zero Stress: AI Meal Planning

Everyday Tech6 min readApril 2025

Key Takeaways

  • An AI tool can generate a full week of meal plans — including a grocery list — in under five minutes
  • You can specify dietary requirements, cooking time limits, and budget constraints upfront
  • The output is a starting point: you'll likely adjust a few meals to suit your tastes
  • AI meal planning works best when you give it detailed constraints, not open-ended requests
  • You don't need a specialist app — a general AI chat tool handles this task well
  • The main limitation is that AI doesn't know what's actually in your fridge or on offer at your local shop

Deciding what to eat is one of the most mundane sources of stress in an average week. By Thursday evening, the question "what shall we have tomorrow?" has lost most of its charm. AI meal planning doesn't make cooking interesting — but it removes the decision fatigue that precedes it.

What AI Can Do Here

A general AI chat tool — Claude, ChatGPT, or Gemini — can produce a seven-day meal plan, complete with ingredients and a categorised shopping list, in the time it takes to make a cup of tea. It can account for dietary requirements, time constraints, budget ranges, and the number of people eating.

The output isn't magic. It's a structured plan that covers all the decisions you'd otherwise have to make individually, spread across a week. The value is that it happens once, at the start of the week, rather than being resolved piecemeal every day.

How to Get a Useful Result

The quality of what you get back depends almost entirely on the quality of what you put in. A vague request produces a vague plan. A specific one produces something actionable.

What a Useful Prompt Looks Like

"Please create a 7-day weeknight dinner plan for two adults. We want meals that take no longer than 40 minutes to cook from scratch. One of us is vegetarian; the other eats fish and poultry but not red meat. We have a weekly grocery budget of around £60. We prefer warming, comfort-food style meals over salads. Please include a shopping list organised by supermarket section."

A prompt like that typically produces a usable plan on the first attempt. You'll probably swap out one or two meals — perhaps the AI suggested something you dislike, or a recipe that requires an ingredient you'd struggle to find — but the heavy lifting is done.

The Shopping List Part

This is where the time saving compounds. Once a meal plan is confirmed, you can ask the AI to generate a shopping list grouped by category: produce, dairy, tins and dry goods, proteins, and so on. This is the kind of organising work that takes five to ten minutes when done manually and about thirty seconds when done through a chat prompt.

You can then copy and paste the list directly into a notes app or your phone's grocery list. Some people paste it into Google Keep or Apple Notes; others use apps like Bring! that let you tick items off as you shop. Either works.

"The goal isn't to eat perfectly. It's to eliminate the moment on Wednesday evening when nobody knows what's for dinner and it's already half past six."

What AI Doesn't Know

Worth being direct about the gaps. An AI has no knowledge of what's currently in your fridge, what's on offer at your nearest supermarket this week, or what you happened to buy in excess last time. It can't account for the three tins of chickpeas sitting in your cupboard.

A useful habit is to start your prompt with a brief inventory: "I already have: one tin of tomatoes, half a bag of pasta, some frozen spinach, and eggs. Please work these into the plan where possible." This takes thirty seconds and meaningfully improves the result.

AI meal plans also don't account for the day when you're too tired to cook anything that takes forty minutes. Building in one or two "emergency" meals — pasta with shop-bought sauce, scrambled eggs on toast — gives the plan a realistic buffer.

Specialist Apps vs General AI Tools

There are apps built specifically for meal planning — Mealime, Paprika, and Whisk are well-regarded examples. These have their own libraries of recipes and can sync directly with online grocery ordering. They're worth exploring if meal planning is a persistent friction point in your week.

But for most people starting out, there's no need to add another app. A general AI chat tool does the job well enough, costs nothing extra, and requires no account setup or subscription beyond what you may already have.

The appeal of AI meal planning isn't that it makes food decisions for you — it's that it removes a low-stakes, repetitive task from the week's mental load. That kind of friction reduction, applied to the right areas of daily life, adds up to something genuinely useful over time.

How to Read a 40‑Page Report in Five Minutes

Everyday Tech5 min readApril 2025

Key Takeaways

  • AI tools can summarise a long document into key points, saving significant reading time
  • You can upload PDFs directly to Claude, ChatGPT, or Gemini and ask specific questions about the content
  • Summarisation is useful for reports, contracts, research papers, and meeting notes
  • AI summaries can miss nuance — for decisions with real consequences, still read the relevant sections
  • You can ask follow-up questions ("what does it say about X?") to probe specific areas without reading everything

Almost everyone has a document they need to understand but doesn't have time to read in full. A long contract. A quarterly report. A research paper for a meeting you're sitting in tomorrow morning. AI summarisation tools exist for exactly this situation — and they're more useful than most people realise.

What Document Summarisation Actually Means

When an AI tool summarises a document, it reads the full text and produces a condensed version — pulling out the main points, key findings, and any conclusions or recommendations. For a 40-page report, this typically produces two to four paragraphs of output that cover what you'd need to know to discuss the document intelligently.

This isn't the same as having read the report. It's the equivalent of having a well-briefed colleague walk you through it before a meeting. Useful — sometimes very useful — but with limits that are worth understanding.

How to Do It

The most direct method: upload the document to an AI chat tool and ask your question. Claude (claude.ai), ChatGPT, and Google Gemini all accept PDF uploads on their free tiers, with some file-size restrictions.

You open the tool, attach the document, and type your question. Something like: "Please summarise the key findings and recommendations from this report in plain language. I have about five minutes before a meeting." The AI will read the document and respond accordingly.

Better Questions Get Better Results

Open-ended "summarise this" requests produce general summaries. If you need specific information, ask for it specifically:

  • "What does this report recommend about staffing levels in Section 4?"
  • "Are there any risks or caveats mentioned? Please list them."
  • "What is the main conclusion, and what evidence does it use to reach it?"
  • "Summarise the financial projections in a format suitable for a non-financial audience."

You can also use the AI as a document search tool: "Does this contract mention anything about early termination fees?" This is significantly faster than CTRL+F for complex or ambiguous language.

For Meeting Notes and Long Threads

Summarisation isn't just for formal documents. It works equally well for long email chains, meeting transcripts, Slack threads exported as text, or research notes that have grown unwieldy over time. Paste the text directly into the chat window if there's no file to upload, and ask the same questions.

Some people use this at the end of a long working week to create a brief record of what was discussed and decided. Others use it to prepare for calls by summarising background documents sent in advance.

"The question isn't whether you should read the document. Sometimes you should. The question is whether you need to read all of it, right now, before you've had any sense of what matters."

Where the Limits Are

AI summaries flatten nuance. A report might contain one important qualification buried in paragraph twelve — a "this only applies if…" or a "however, in certain circumstances…" — that changes the meaning of the headline finding. A summary may not preserve this with enough prominence.

For anything with significant consequences — a legal contract you're about to sign, a medical document, a financial agreement — use AI summarisation to orient yourself, then read the relevant sections fully before making any decision. Summaries help you know where to look. They don't replace looking.

File size is also a practical constraint. Most free tiers handle documents up to 20–30 pages reliably. Very long documents (100+ pages) may need to be summarised in sections, or may require a paid subscription for full processing.

One Genuinely Underused Technique

After reading the AI's summary, ask: "What questions should I be asking about this document that I haven't asked yet?" This prompt often surfaces issues or angles the summary didn't mention — things the AI identified as potentially significant but didn't include in the headline points. It takes thirty seconds and frequently produces something worth knowing.

There are more documents in a working life than there is time to read them carefully. AI summarisation doesn't change that reality — but it does give you a more practical way to navigate it, so that the time you do spend reading is spent on the parts that actually require your full attention.
